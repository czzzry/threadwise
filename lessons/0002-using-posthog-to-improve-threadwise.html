Lesson 0002

PostHog is Threadwise's product feedback loop

The goal is not to admire a dashboard. The goal is to see what happened in the real review workflow, make a product diagnosis, change one thing, and learn whether it helped.

Open the operating sheet Open the Threadwise dashboard

Mission tie-in

You want to reason about Threadwise like a professional product engineer. Analytics is how you move from “this felt weird” to “this step is repeatedly failing, so here is the next bounded change.”

What happens when you use Threadwise

1

You act normally

Open the sidebar, enter the needs-attention queue, review an email, accept or refine a proposal, choose the scope, and let Threadwise write the label.

2

Threadwise emits small receipts

Each meaningful transition becomes an event such as review queue opened, suggestion decision made, or label write completed.

3

PostHog aggregates receipts

Trends compare counts and outcomes over time. Funnels attempt to show where a sequence stops. Breakdowns split a total by properties such as decision type or retry outcome.

4

You choose the next product change

A repeated pattern becomes a hypothesis: perhaps the queue is hard to enter, proposals require too many edits, or Gmail writes are unreliable.

Events are receipts, not surveillance. A receipt says that one meaningful thing happened and carries a few fields that help aggregate it.

Time to first writeHow long from opening to a successful write?Directional only until reviews are grouped into repeated anonymous sessions.

The one-user analytics trap

PostHog funnels normally reason about people moving through steps. Threadwise currently has one founder and one stable anonymous installation ID.

That means a person-level funnel can collapse many review attempts into one “person converted” result. It cannot, by itself, tell you that 17 of 25 review sessions reached a successful write.

Recommendation: add a fresh anonymous review_session_id for each queue-review run, propagate it to the authoritative write event, and aggregate funnels by that session.

PostHog recommends starting funnels with the first action in a flow and ending with the success action, while keeping the flow simple. It also offers “time to convert” for finding high-friction steps. See the official funnels guide.

How I recommend you use it

For 1–2 weeks

Turn production analytics on and use Threadwise normally. Do not change behavior to feed the dashboard.

Once a week

Spend ten minutes reading reliability, use, quality, and speed—in that order.

Choose one hypothesis

Write one sentence: “I think X happens because Y.” Avoid changing three things at once.

Compare later

Use trends to see whether event counts or breakdowns moved after the change. PostHog describes trends as the tool for monitoring important metrics and comparing usage over time.

Your weekly reading order

R-U-Q-S

Reliability: Did writes succeed? Did retries recover?

Use: Did you enter the queue, start reviews, and finish batches?

Quality: Did you approve, edit, or reject what Threadwise proposed?

Speed: How long did decisions and successful outcomes take?

How to turn a chart into a decision

Observation

“Reviews start, but edits are common and batches rarely complete.”

Hypothesis

“Rule proposals are too broad, so every email becomes a negotiation.”

Bounded change

“Default to current-email scope and make broader rules a secondary action.”

What I would add next

A

Review session identity

A random per-review identifier makes repeated single-user funnels and duration analysis legitimate.

B

Separate classification from rule decisions

Record whether the proposed label was approved, edited, or rejected separately from whether a broader reusable rule was accepted.

C

Add aggregatable quality properties

Track proposed label, final label, suggestion source, and confidence bucket. This reveals which categories and mechanisms are weak.

Why not send whole emails? Not because we need to be precious about privacy. Because bodies, subjects, and prose are nearly unique values. PostHog cannot turn that high-cardinality soup into a useful chart. Keep the content local; send the categories needed for comparison.

Retrieval practice

Answer before rereading:

  1. What is the difference between an event, an insight, and a dashboard?
  2. Why is the current person-level activation funnel weak for a one-user product?
  3. Which metric should you inspect before proposal quality or speed?
  4. If edits rise, what can you conclude—and what can you not conclude?
  5. What three properties would most improve classification-quality analysis?
